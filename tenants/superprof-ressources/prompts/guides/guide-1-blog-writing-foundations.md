# What We Create & Why

Superprof operates in more than 60 countries, each with its own blog. Our largest markets drive the strategy forward and produce most of the original content: France, Spain, Italy, Brazil and UK (with our other English speaking markets).

The other markets mainly translate and adapt this content for their local audience.

**Our blogs exist to:**
- Provide readers with clear, useful, well-researched content
- Establish Superprof as a trusted voice in education
- Help people discover that private tutoring can support their learning goals
- Drive organic search traffic through quality content

We succeed when readers find genuine value in what we write. We inform first, inspire second, and trust that quality content naturally leads to conversions.

## Types of articles

We have 3 different types of orders. The core writing principles and WordPress requirements apply to all of them.

- **Original** — Writing from scratch based on a brief. You research, structure, and create all content.
- **Refresh** — Improving an existing article. Your brief specifies what needs to change: adding content, restructuring, updating visuals, or optimising for new keywords.
- **Translation** — Translating content from another market. You translate and localise—adapting tone, examples, statistics, and references to fit your country cultural context.

# Our Voice & Tone

We write as people who understand education from multiple perspectives. We have been students navigating confusing concepts. We have been teachers preparing lessons and adapting to different learners. This shared experience shapes how we communicate.

**WE ARE**
- **Truthful** — We present balanced information. We help readers weigh advantages and disadvantages so they can make their own choices. We do not exaggerate or mislead.
- **Ambitious** — We believe in setting high goals. We want our readers to feel that growth is possible. Our writing should energise, not discourage.
- **Understanding** — We appreciate that everyone is different. We do not judge choices, preferences, or learning paths. We welcome all backgrounds.

**OUR TONE**

Our tone is **natural** and **tends toward informal**.
Learning happens everywhere: in classrooms, at home, on beaches, in mountains. We **guide readers to understanding**. We do not talk down to them. We are conversational without being careless. If subtle humour fits naturally, use it. But always **stay respectful** and avoid anything that could exclude readers. Our users and readers come first. We always consider their perspective when we write.

Our aim is to **inspire people to learn and grow**, not to discourage them. Even when discussing challenges or gaps in knowledge, maintain an **encouraging perspective**. Learning is a journey. Everyone starts somewhere.

### Writing about other people

**AGE** shouldn't matter in learning and so we don't speak of it unless it is relevant to our article.

**GENDER** is generally not relevant in learning maths or foreign languages. Rather than writing elaborate inclusive constructions, restructure sentences to avoid gendered pronouns entirely. This produces cleaner, more accessible text and helps readers feel included without effort.

### Writing about other brands

When referring to brand names in your writing, it's important to use the exact spelling and capitalisation as the brand itself uses on their official website. This maintains accuracy and professionalism.
For example, use 'YouTube', not 'youtube'.

### Writing about Superprof

We are **Superprof**. Not Super Prof. Not SuperProf. Not SP.

Superprof is not "he" or "she."

The name may suggest "superhero," but we do not emphasise this. Through our work, we honour all education heroes—past and present—and hope readers feel inspired to become one too.

# Writing for our readers

Our readers come to learn. They want answers quickly and clearly. Write so that anyone can understand, regardless of their background or expertise level.

### Keep it accessible

- Write short sentences: aim for under 20 words
- Express one idea per sentence
- Use active voice: "Tutors create lesson plans" not "Lesson plans are created by tutors"
- Group sentences into paragraphs of 2-5 sentences
- Use precise vocabulary that your audience will understand.

### Working with translations

At Superprof, translation is a strategic tool. It helps us optimise editorial efforts by reusing high-performing content across multiple countries, expanding its reach without duplicating work. A well-translated article allows us to connect with local audiences while ensuring consistency in brand voice and messaging.

Translation also helps us make valuable content accessible globally—supporting our mission to inspire and inform learners and tutors wherever they are.

### Know Your Audience

A translation should always consider the people it is intended for. Each local version of Superprof has its own readership, shaped by cultural context, local education systems, and content expectations. Understanding these differences is essential for making translated articles feel organic rather than foreign.

- **Tone of voice**: The tone must reflect both the original content and local expectations. For instance, an article written with a playful tone in French may need to be adjusted to feel equally engaging in German or Italian, where different stylistic conventions apply.
- **Cultural context**: Educational references, school systems, exams, and even casual examples (like names or local customs) should be localised to maintain relevance.
- **Reader expectations**: What works well for a UK audience might not resonate in Spain or Brazil. Adapting content to match what readers are searching for—both in terms of language and intention—is key to maintaining performance.

### Frame things positively

Choose sentence structures that focus on possibility rather than limitation.

> ✅ **DO**
> Most finance students find it easier to grasp concepts when supported by a private tutor.

> 🚫 **DO NOT**
> Most finance students don't fully understand lessons and need to pay for private tutoring.

Both sentences convey similar information, but the first frames tutoring as an opportunity. The second frames it as a remedy for failure.

### Make content skimmable

Online readers scan before they read. Help them find what they need quickly.

- Use headings that clearly describe what follows
- Put important information early in sections
- Use bold sparingly to highlight key terms (not entire sentences)
- Break up dense information with bullet points, tables, or numbered lists
- Place images and visual elements where they support the text
- Use mandatory Gutenberg blocks

### Write strong introductions

Introductions should be direct and engaging. Make readers want to continue. Keep introductions to 1-2 paragraphs maximum. Get to the substance quickly.

# Understanding Keywords and Links

Before you start writing, you need to understand two things:
1. Why some keywords are off-limits for blog articles
2. Why linking between articles matters

**What is anchor text?**

Anchor text is the clickable text in a hyperlink. In this sentence: "Learn more about [different types of yoga.](https://www.superprof.co.uk/blog/learn-yoga-forms/)" → "different types of yoga" is the anchor text. These words tell search engines what the linked page is about. Choosing the right anchor text matters for search rankings.

Not all keywords work the same way. Superprof divides keywords into three types:

1. **💰 Money keywords**: are short, high-value search terms like "piano lessons," "maths tutor," or "English tutor London." These are reserved for landing pages because they attract people ready to book a tutor.
2. **☀️ Warm keywords**: are longer phrases that still show interest in finding a tutor, such as "how to find a good piano teacher" or "best way to learn Spanish."
3. **🥶 Cold keywords**: are informational phrases where people are researching, not buying. Examples: "benefits of learning a second language" or "how long does it take to learn guitar."

**Why this matters for your writing**

Superprof has two types of pages:
- **Landing pages** (URLs containing `/lessons/`) — Built to rank for money keywords
- **Blog articles** (URLs containing `/blog/`) — Support landing pages by attracting readers through informational content

**The key rule:** Blog articles should never compete with landing pages for money keywords.

As a writer, you will only work with links to other blog articles. Here is what you need to remember:

| When linking to... | Use this type of anchor text | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Another blog article | Warm or cold keywords only | "benefits of learning English" |
| A landing page | Money keywords | "English tutor London" |

**Never use money keywords as anchor text when linking to blog articles.** This protects our landing pages from internal competition.

# Quality Standards

Every piece of content should meet these standards:

**Structured** — Content is well organised. Readers can grasp information quickly through clear headings and logical flow.

**Clear** — Writing is easy to understand and free of unnecessary jargon. Logic and structure make content scannable.

**Concise** — Every sentence earns its place. We avoid repetition and filler. If a sentence does not add value, we remove it.

**Useful** — Content answers a genuine need. We do not pad articles with irrelevant information.

**Friendly** — We use a warm, approachable tone. This reflects who we are: a multicultural company where people from many backgrounds work and learn together.

**Appropriate** — We consider our audience and write as we would speak to them. We adapt our level of detail and formality to match what readers need.

### What quality content looks like

Good content is:
1. **Well-researched** — claims are accurate and supported
2. **Comprehensive** — covers the topic thoroughly without padding
3. **Relevant** — directly addresses what readers searched for
4. **Clear and engaging** — holds attention through good writing
5. **Focused** — stays on topic without tangents
6. **Logically organised** — follows a structure that makes sense

### What to avoid

- **Controversial subjects** — We do not take political positions or discuss divisive topics unnecessarily
- **Sensational language** — Words like "amazing" or "incredible" belong in product reviews, not educational content
- **Irrelevant tangents** — Stay focused on what readers came to learn
- **Information that does not apply locally** — Always check whether examples, statistics, or references work for your specific country

# Our AI guidelines

We embrace AI as a tool but set clear boundaries.

> **Good AI use**
> - Finding inspiration and generating ideas
> - Improving article structure
> - Refining titles or calls to action
> - Checking grammar and clarity

> **Do not**
> - Copy and paste AI-generated text directly into articles
> - Let AI replace your voice and judgment
> - Skip the work of understanding your topic

At the core of our blog is authentic, human-created content. AI can help you write better, but the ideas, examples, and perspective should come from you.

We conduct random checks on articles using tools that detect AI-generated text and plagiarism. All published content must meet our standards for originality.

# Guidelines on images

Choose images with meaning, not decoration. Every image should contribute something to the reader's understanding.

### Principles

- **Purpose over aesthetics** — Ask yourself what the image adds. If the answer is only "it looks nice," reconsider.
- **Natural over stock** — Prefer authentic-looking photos from sources like Unsplash. Avoid overly staged or caricatured images.
- **Context through captions** — Good captions tell readers what they are seeing: the location, subject, and photographer when relevant.

### Examples of meaningful image choices

If writing about private tutoring history, consider an image of Confucius—history's first private professor—and explain the connection in your text.

If writing about the Olympic Games, an image of ancient Olympia's ruins adds historical context that a generic sports photo cannot.

Images should support your text, not replace explanation. A meaningful caption transforms decoration into information.
